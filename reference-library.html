📜 Apocryphal & Second Temple Texts

These texts are not canonical Scripture in most Protestant traditions, but they are significant historical and theological documents — especially for understanding what Jewish communities believed in the centuries before Christ. The Dead Sea Scroll community (Essenes) treated the Book of Jubilees and 1 Enoch as authoritative alongside Torah.

Apocryphal Text
The Book of Jubilees
A retelling of Genesis through Exodus 14, organized around 49-year Jubilee cycles. Explicitly commands the 364-day solar calendar and declares that using the lunar calendar corrupts the feasts and Sabbaths. Considered authoritative at Qumran. Key passages: Jubilees 6:36–38 (the 364-day year).
📌 Studied in: Theme 2, Module 3 — Referenced in: Theme 2 Modules 1–2, Theme 1 Module 1

Apocryphal Text
1 Enoch (Book of Enoch)
Jewish apocalyptic writings attributed to the biblical Enoch (Genesis 5:18–24). Sections include the Book of the Watchers, the Astronomical Book (calendar), and the Book of Parables (messianic Son of Man). Jude 14 quotes 1 Enoch directly. Key calendar passage: 1 Enoch 72:32. Enoch 72:1 states original cycles do not change until the new creation.
📌 Studied in: Theme 2, Modules 1–2 — Referenced in: Theme 1 Module 1
Read Online →
Deuterocanonical Text
1 & 2 Maccabees
Historical accounts of the Maccabean revolt against Antiochus IV Epiphanes. 1 Maccabees covers the military story; 2 Maccabees provides theological reflection. Critical background for Daniel's "abomination of desolation," Matthew 24, and the Zadokite priestly crisis. 2 Maccabees 4 specifically documents the removal of Onias III.

🗺️ Dead Sea Scrolls & Qumran Texts

Discovered at Qumran between 1947 and 1956, the Dead Sea Scrolls are the oldest known manuscripts of the Hebrew Bible along with a library of documents produced by the Essene community. They are directly relevant to both the calendar study (Theme 2) and the prophetic timeline study (Theme 1).

Digital Archive
The Leon Levy Dead Sea Scrolls Digital Library
High-resolution images of the actual Dead Sea Scroll fragments with English translation tools. Browse the calendar texts (4Q318, 4Q394), the Community Rule, and the Damascus Document. Hosted by the Israel Antiquities Authority.
📌 Referenced in: Theme 2, Modules 1–2
Browse Scrolls →
Dead Sea Scroll Document
The Damascus Document (Zadokite Fragments)
One of the most important Essene documents — a community covenant text outlining the founding of the Qumran community and its identity as the faithful remnant. Calls members "sons of Zadok." Explicitly condemns the corruption of the priesthood under the Hellenized high priests. Two medieval copies were also found in a Cairo synagogue in 1896.
📌 Referenced in: Theme 2, Module 2 (Calendar Timeline — Zadokite section)
Read Online →
Dead Sea Scroll Document
The Community Rule (1QS — Manual of Discipline)
The governing charter of the Essene community at Qumran. Contains the famous "Two Spirits" teaching — the Spirit of Truth (Sons of Light) vs. the Spirit of Falsehood (Sons of Darkness) — language that closely parallels John's Gospel and 1 John. Outlines 364-day calendar observances the community followed.

🏛️ Historical Background

Key historical sources for understanding the inter-testamental period, the Jewish sects, and the political context that shapes Daniel's prophecies and the New Testament.

First-Century Jewish Historian
Josephus — Antiquities of the Jews & The Jewish War
Flavius Josephus (37–100 AD) is the primary first-century Jewish historian. Antiquities of the Jews covers Creation through Nero; The Jewish War documents the revolt and destruction of Jerusalem in 70 AD. Essential for the historical fulfillment of Matthew 24 prophecies and for understanding the Pharisee, Sadducee, and Essene sects.

Historical Figure
Antiochus IV Epiphanes — Historical Background
Antiochus IV Epiphanes (175–164 BC) is one of the most significant figures in prophetic history. Daniel 11 describes him as a type of the future Antichrist. He desecrated the Jerusalem Temple, sacrificed a pig on the altar, forced the Seleucid lunar calendar on Jewish worship, and displaced the Zadokite high priest — the historical template for the "abomination of desolation."

⚜️ The Zadokite Priests

The Zadokite priestly line is a key thread connecting the Old Testament priesthood, the Essenes, the Dead Sea Scrolls, the calendar controversy, and the corruption that Daniel and Revelation warn about. Understanding who they were unlocks context across multiple modules.

Priestly Lineage
Who Were the Zadokite Priests?
The Zadokites (Sons of Zadok) held the Jerusalem high priesthood in an unbroken line for roughly 800 years — from Solomon's appointment of Zadok (~950 BC) through 175 BC. They were guardians of both the Temple rituals and the sacred 364-day solar calendar, which they believed was divinely revealed. When Antiochus IV removed Onias III (replaced first by Jason through bribery, then by Menelaus — who wasn't even a Zadokite), the faithful Zadokites withdrew to Qumran with the Temple library — producing the Dead Sea Scrolls.
📌 Studied in: Theme 2, Module 2 (Calendar Timeline) — Referenced in: Theme 2, Module 1
See Theme 2 Module 2 →
Historical Event
The Fall of the Zadokite Priesthood (175 BC)
In 175 BC, the legitimate Zadokite high priest Onias III was removed when his own brother Jason bribed Antiochus IV for the position. Then Menelaus — not even from the Zadokite line — outbid Jason, and Onias III was assassinated. The Essenes identified this as the beginning of catastrophic corruption of the Temple and the calendar. Documented in 2 Maccabees 4.
