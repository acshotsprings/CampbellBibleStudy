Bible Translations

Each translation has a different purpose. Knowing when to reach for which one sharpens your study. The NLT is the default for this guide.

NLT Default
New Living Translation
Thought-for-thought translation in modern English. Prioritizes clarity and readability while staying faithful to the original meaning. Excellent for reading large passages and for devotional study.
Used throughout this study guide as the default Scripture translation.
ESV
English Standard Version
Word-for-word translation that balances accuracy with readability. More literal than NLT. Widely trusted for in-depth study, memorization, and theological precision. Good for cross-checking key prophetic passages.
Best for: Comparing key prophetic words and phrases against the NLT.
KJV
King James Version
The 1611 Authorized Version. Highly literal, formal, and poetic. Many classic prophecy teachers (Walvoord, Lindsey, Scofield) quote KJV. Useful for tracing how traditional prophetic interpretation was built.
Best for: Cross-referencing classic commentaries and traditional prophetic teaching.
NASB
New American Standard Bible
One of the most literal word-for-word translations available. Preferred by serious students and seminaries for its precision. Can feel stiff to read but excellent for word studies.
Best for: Word-level accuracy when studying Daniel, Ezekiel, and Revelation passages.
NIV
New International Version
A balance of accuracy and readability. One of the most widely read modern translations. The 2011 revision updated some gendered language. Broadly accessible and widely quoted in contemporary teaching.
Best for: General reading and contemporary teaching cross-reference.
MSG
The Message
A paraphrase by Eugene Peterson — not a formal translation. Renders Scripture in very contemporary, conversational language. Not recommended for detailed doctrinal study but can bring fresh perspective to familiar passages.
Best for: Devotional reading — not for doctrinal or prophetic precision.
AMP
Amplified Bible
Expands on key words and phrases using brackets to show the full range of meanings in the original Hebrew and Greek. Useful for seeing how much is packed into a single word in prophecy passages.
Best for: Understanding the depth of specific prophetic words and phrases.
LXX
Septuagint (Greek OT)
The Greek translation of the Hebrew Old Testament used by the early church and quoted extensively in the New Testament. Important for understanding how Ezekiel's prophecies were read in the first century — including by the apostles.
Best for: Tracing OT prophecy as understood by NT writers and early church.

Study Tools & Resources

Online Tool
BibleGateway.com
Search any verse across 60+ translations simultaneously. Excellent for side-by-side translation comparisons. Has audio Bible, reading plans, and commentaries.
→ Use for translation comparison on key prophecy passages
Online Tool
BlueLetter Bible (blueletterbible.org)
Free tool for original language word studies — look up Hebrew and Greek words, Strong's numbers, interlinear text. Essential when you want to go deeper on a specific word in Daniel or Ezekiel.
→ Use for Hebrew word studies in Ezekiel 38 (Rosh, Magog, Gog)
Online Tool
AllIsrael.com / Joel Rosenberg's Blog
Rosenberg's ongoing news and commentary site. Tracks current events in Israel and the Middle East through a prophetic lens. Directly relevant to tracking the Gog-Magog coalition forming in real time.
→ Live source for Checklist Phase 1 current events
Reference
Strong's Exhaustive Concordance
The definitive index to every word in the Bible with Hebrew and Greek definitions. Available free online or as a physical book. Foundational for word-level Bible study.
→ Use alongside BlueLetter Bible for word studies
