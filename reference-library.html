Ancient Texts

Second Temple and inter-testamental texts that provide historical and prophetic context. These are not Scripture but are valuable for understanding the world in which the biblical authors wrote. PDFs are stored in your resources folder.

📜 The Book of Enoch
1 Enoch — Standard English Version, translated by Jay Winter · ~2nd–1st century BC · Second Temple Jewish text
An ancient Jewish apocalyptic text attributed to Enoch (Genesis 5:24). Predates the New Testament and was widely read in the Second Temple period — fragments found among the Dead Sea Scrolls. Contains visions of fallen angels (the Watchers), the coming judgment, the Messiah figure ("the Son of Man"), and detailed astronomical/calendar material relevant to Theme 2. Quoted directly in the New Testament (Jude 1:14–15). Not canonical but illuminating for prophetic background.
→ Theme 2 (Biblical Calendar — 364-day solar year), Module 9 (angelic warfare background), Jude 1:14–15

📜 The Book of Jubilees
R.H. Charles translation · ~2nd century BC · Second Temple Jewish text · Found in Dead Sea Scrolls
A retelling of Genesis and Exodus organized around a 49-year jubilee cycle. Presents history as unfolding in precise divine time units — weeks of years, jubilees of jubilees. Insists on a 364-day solar calendar and condemns the lunar calendar as corrupting God's appointed times. Fragments found in the Dead Sea Scrolls. Foundational for understanding the Essene calendar conviction and its prophetic implications. Not canonical but historically significant — the Qumran community treated it as authoritative.
→ Theme 2 Module 3 (Book of Jubilees dedicated module), Daniel's 70 Weeks (Module 1), Theme 2 Module 1 (Calendar History)

Bible Translations

Each translation serves a different purpose. The NLT is the default for this guide.

NLT Default
New Living Translation
Thought-for-thought in modern English. Prioritizes clarity and readability. Excellent for large passages and devotional reading.
Used throughout this study guide as the default.
ESV
English Standard Version
Word-for-word, balancing accuracy with readability. Widely trusted for in-depth study. Good for cross-checking key prophetic passages.
Best for: Comparing key prophetic words against the NLT.
KJV
King James Version
The 1611 Authorized Version. Highly literal and poetic. Classic prophecy teachers (Walvoord, Lindsey, Scofield) quote KJV.
Best for: Cross-referencing classic commentaries.
NASB
New American Standard Bible
One of the most literal translations available. Preferred by seminaries for precision. Excellent for word studies.
Best for: Word-level accuracy in Daniel, Ezekiel, Revelation.
NIV
New International Version
Balance of accuracy and readability. One of the most widely read modern translations. Broadly accessible.
Best for: General reading and contemporary teaching.
AMP
Amplified Bible
Expands key words using brackets to show the full range of Hebrew and Greek meaning. Useful for prophetic word depth.
Best for: Understanding depth in specific prophetic words.
LXX
Septuagint (Greek OT)
Greek translation of the Hebrew OT used by the early church and quoted in the New Testament. Important for how Ezekiel was read in the first century.
Best for: Tracing OT prophecy as read by NT writers.
