H7620 — What Does "Weeks" Really Mean in Daniel 9?

Strong's H7620 — shabua (שָׁבוּעַ)
Pronunciation: shaw-BOO-ah
Literal meaning: "sevened" — a unit of seven. Not automatically days. Context determines what it is seven of.
Strong's definition: "properly passive participle of H7650 as a denominative of H7651; literally sevened, that is, a week (specifically of years)"
Where to find it: Page 293 of the Complete KJV Strong's Hebrew Concordance in your Resource Library. Search for H7620 — not the transliteration spelling, but the number.

Four Converging Lines of Evidence

Line 1 — Daniel 10 Proves He Knew How to Specify Days
Daniel 10:2–3: Daniel mourns for "three full weeks" — in Hebrew: shavuim yamim — "weeks of DAYS." He adds the word yamim (days) to make it unambiguous.
Daniel 9:24: "Seventy weeks are decreed" — in Hebrew: shivim shabuim — NO word for days. Completely absent.
Conclusion: Daniel knew exactly how to write "seven-day weeks" when he meant them. In chapter 9 he deliberately left "days" out. That is not accident — it signals a different unit of seven.
Line 2 — The Masculine Plural Grammar Signal
Normally the plural of "week" in Hebrew is shavuot — a feminine ending. In Daniel 9 it uses the masculine plural shabuim — the same ending used for shanim (years).
This grammatical signal, unique to Daniel 9 in all of the Old Testament, points a Hebrew reader toward years, not days. It is one of four lines of evidence — but it is not accidental.
Line 3 — The Day-Year Principle: Two Explicit Biblical Foundations
Numbers 14:34: God tells Israel they will wander 40 years — "one year for each of the 40 days" the spies explored Canaan. Day = Year, stated explicitly by God.
Ezekiel 4:6: God tells Ezekiel to lie on his side a set number of days — "I have assigned you one day for each year." Day = Year, stated explicitly by God.
These are not interpretive suggestions — they are explicit divine statements. The principle is built into Scripture by God Himself.
Line 4 — The Mathematical Impossibility of Literal Weeks
If weeks = literal 7-day weeks: 70 × 7 = 490 days = just over one year. Impossible to fit the rebuilding of Jerusalem, the arrival of Messiah, and the destruction of the Temple into one year.
If weeks = 7-year periods: 70 × 7 = 490 years. Starting from Artaxerxes' decree (444 BC), 483 years (69 weeks) = AD 33 — the year of Jesus's triumphal entry into Jerusalem. Fulfilled to the year.
The 70th week: The final 7-year period = the Tribulation. Still future. The gap between week 69 and week 70 = the Church Age we are in now.

The Jewish "Week of Years" Was Not a Foreign Concept

The ancient Jewish people had "sevens of years" built into their law and culture. They farmed for six years and let the land rest on the seventh — the Sabbatical Year (Lev 25). Seven times seven years = 49 years, with the 50th being the Jubilee. The high priest even asked Alexander the Great for tax relief every seventh year.
A "week of years" = seven years was completely natural to any Jewish person hearing Daniel's prophecy. The English word "weeks" forces a meaning onto the text that the original Hebrew does not require.
