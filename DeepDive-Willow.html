🌿 The Willow in Scripture

Dedicated to Hazlee Willow Rogers — whose name carried her grandfather into a deeper study of God's Word.

🔗 Cross-References
• Sermon Log #11 (4/6/26) — Feast of Tabernacles & DSS Calendar (Ken Johnson) — source of this study
• Leviticus 23:40 — the Four Species commandment
• Psalm 137:1–2 · Isaiah 44:3–4 · Ezekiel 17:5 · Job 40:22

During the study of the Feast of Tabernacles and the Four Species (Sermon Log #11, April 6, 2026), the willow emerged as the most unexpected of the four plants. In Jewish tradition the willow has no taste and no fragrance — representing a person with neither Torah knowledge nor good deeds. Yet it is bound together with the other three on Sukkot, and the bundle is incomplete without it. That observation opened a much deeper question: what does Scripture actually say about the willow — and why does God keep returning to it?

The Four Species — Sukkot (Leviticus 23:40)
Etrog (citron): Has taste AND fragrance → one with Torah knowledge AND good deeds
Lulav (palm branch): Has taste but no fragrance → Torah knowledge without good deeds
Myrtle: Has fragrance but no taste → good deeds without Torah knowledge
Willow (aravah): No taste, no fragrance → neither Torah knowledge nor good deeds
Yet all four are bound together. The bundle is only complete when the willow is included. The least becomes essential to the whole.

The Willow Across Scripture

Psalm 137:1–2 — The Willow of Exile
"By the rivers of Babylon we sat and wept when we remembered Zion. There on the poplars (willows) we hung our harps."
The willow here is the tree of grief, of longing, of songs silenced by captivity. Israel hung their harps — their worship — on the willows because they could not sing in a foreign land. The willow becomes the keeper of suspended praise. It holds what cannot yet be expressed.
In the last days, Israel's full worship will be restored. The harps will come down from the willows.
Isaiah 44:3–4 — The Willow of Spiritual Renewal
"For I will pour water on the thirsty land, and streams on the dry ground; I will pour my Spirit upon your offspring and my blessing on your descendants. They shall spring up among the grass like willows by flowing streams."
Here the willow is the picture of revival — rapid, abundant growth where the Spirit is poured out. Willows grow faster than almost any tree when water is present. God uses the willow to describe what His Spirit does in a dry and thirsty generation: explosive, visible, unstoppable growth.
The outpouring of the Spirit in the last days (Joel 2:28–29, Acts 2:17) is described in willow language — growth along streams of living water.
Ezekiel 17:5 — The Willow of Covenant
"He took of the seed of the land and planted it in fertile soil. He placed it beside abundant waters. He set it like a willow twig."
In Ezekiel's parable of two eagles and a vine, God plants Israel like a willow cutting beside abundant waters — representing His covenant commitment to sustain and grow His people even in captivity. The willow twig is not impressive. It is small, easily overlooked. But planted beside water by God's hand, it becomes something that cannot be stopped.
Job 40:22 — The Willow of Creation Order
"The lotus trees cover it for shade; the willows of the brook surround it."
God describes Behemoth — His most powerful creature — resting in the shade of willows. The willow frames the natural order God created and sustains. Even the mightiest creature in creation rests beneath it. The willow is not fragile — it is the canopy of the strong.

What the Willow Tells Us

1. God includes the least. In Sukkot, the willow with nothing to offer — no taste, no fragrance — is still required. The bundle is incomplete without it. God does not build His kingdom only from the gifted and the notable.
2. The willow holds suspended praise. Psalm 137 — the harps on willows are not gone. They are waiting. There is a season of silence and grief, but the worship will return. The willow keeps it.
3. The willow is the picture of Spirit-filled growth. Isaiah 44 — where the Spirit is poured out, people spring up like willows by water. Fast, visible, unstoppable.
4. The willow survives what other trees cannot. Willows grow in flood plains, in wet soil, in places other trees die. They bend without breaking. They are planted by rivers on purpose — not despite the water, but because of it.
A name given to a granddaughter by parents who didn't know all this — and yet God wove it through His Word from Leviticus to Revelation. That is not coincidence. That is the fingerprint of a Father who names His children before they know their own name.
