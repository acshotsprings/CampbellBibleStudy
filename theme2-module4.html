This module draws from an audio teaching session (April 6, 2026) covering the rituals and prophetic meaning of Yom Kippur and the Feast of Tabernacles (Sukkot). It includes a close look at the Azazel scapegoat ritual, the Four Species, the Seventy Nations offering, the Isavrim water-drawing ceremony, and the Dead Sea Scroll calendar as it relates to these feasts. It also includes a personal special study: the significance of the willow in Scripture — dedicated to Hazelee Willow Rogers.

Part 1 — Yom Kippur: The Two Goats

Yom Kippur — the Day of Atonement — centered on one of the most dramatic rituals in all of Scripture: the two-goat ceremony described in Leviticus 16. The High Priest would bring forward two identical goats — alike in appearance, size, and weight — and cast lots between them. One goat was designated for the Lord and would be slain as a sin offering. The other was designated for Azazel and would be sent alive into the wilderness.

Leviticus 16:7–10 (NASB 1995)
"He shall take the two goats and present them before the LORD at the doorway of the tent of meeting. Aaron shall cast lots for the two goats, one lot for the LORD and the other lot for the scapegoat. Then Aaron shall offer the goat on which the lot for the LORD fell, and make it a sin offering. But the goat on which the lot for the scapegoat fell shall be presented alive before the LORD, to make atonement upon it, to send it into the wilderness as the scapegoat."
Who — or What — Is Azazel?
The word Azazel has been variously translated as "scapegoat," "the wilderness," or "the cliff" — but the teaching in this session emphasized that Azazel is a proper name. It is not merely a place or concept. Jewish tradition — including the Book of Enoch — identifies Azazel as a fallen angelic being to whom sins were symbolically returned. Moses wrote about this ceremony in Leviticus 16, and the Mishnah (tractate Yoma) provides additional detail about how the ritual was performed, including that the goat was pushed backward off a cliff in the wilderness to ensure it could not return.
Note: The identification of Azazel as a proper name is textually supported but involves interpretive judgment. The important theological point is that one goat died bearing sin, and one goat carried sin away — a two-part picture of atonement that points to Christ's completed work: both the sacrifice and the removal of sin.
The Mishnah's Details (Yoma 6)
• The two goats must be alike in appearance, size, and weight — purchased at the same time.
• A piece of red thread (Hebrew: lashon shel zehorit) was tied to one of the horns of the Azazel goat and another piece to the door of the sanctuary. According to the Talmud (Yoma 39b), during the time of Simeon the Just, the thread would turn white as the goat was sent away — a miraculous sign. This practice is believed to have stopped approximately 40 years before the destruction of the Second Temple (c. AD 30 — which corresponds to the year of the crucifixion).
• The goat was led by a designated man who walked it ten stations into the wilderness, then pushed it backward off a cliff.
Isaiah 1:18 (NASB 1995)
"Come now, and let us reason together," says the LORD, "Though your sins are as scarlet, they will be as white as snow; though they are red like crimson, they will be like wool."
🙏 Reflection
The red thread turning white when the goat was sent away — and apparently stopping around AD 30 — is one of the most striking historical footnotes in the rabbinic literature. If that timing is accurate, what does it say about how God may have allowed one system to go silent as the true sacrifice was accomplished?

Part 2 — The Feast of Tabernacles (Sukkot)

Seven days after Yom Kippur begins Sukkot — the Feast of Tabernacles. God commanded Israel to dwell in temporary shelters (sukkot) for seven days to remember the wilderness wanderings, when the Lord tabernacled with His people in the desert. But the feast is also intensely forward-looking: it points to the coming Messianic age when God will once again dwell with His people.

Leviticus 23:33–36, 41–43 (NASB 1995)
Again the LORD spoke to Moses, saying, "Speak to the sons of Israel, saying, 'On the fifteenth of this seventh month is the Feast of Booths for seven days to the LORD. On the first day is a holy convocation; you shall not do any laborious work. For seven days you shall present an offering by fire to the LORD. On the eighth day you shall have a holy convocation and present an offering by fire to the LORD; it is an assembly. You shall not do any laborious work.' ... 'So you shall celebrate it as a feast to the LORD for seven days in the year. It shall be a permanent statute throughout your generations; you shall celebrate it in the seventh month. You shall live in booths for seven days; all the native-born in Israel shall live in booths, so that your generations may know that I had the sons of Israel live in booths when I brought them out from the land of Egypt. I am the LORD your God.'"
The Second Tithe and the Great Feast
Every three years at Tabernacles, Israel was commanded to bring the second tithe — a tithe of the tithe — for a great feast. This was not a solemn, penitential gathering but a celebration: eat, drink, and rejoice before the Lord with your family and all in your household. The feast was to reflect abundance and covenant blessing — a foretaste of the Messianic banquet.
The Seventy Nations Offering
Numbers 29 prescribes the unique burnt offerings for Tabernacles — a total of seventy bulls over the seven days (13 on day 1, decreasing by one each day through day 7). The Talmud (Sukkah 55b) records that the rabbis understood these seventy bulls as offerings for the seventy nations of the world — the full table of nations from Genesis 10. Tabernacles was not just Israel's feast; it was a feast on behalf of all humanity. This is confirmed by Zechariah 14, where the nations that survive the Day of the Lord come up to Jerusalem annually to observe Sukkot during the Millennium.
Zechariah 14:16 (NASB 1995)
Then it will come about that any who are left of all the nations that went against Jerusalem will go up from year to year to worship the King, the LORD of hosts, and to celebrate the Feast of Booths.
🙏 Reflection
Tabernacles is one of the only feasts explicitly prophesied to be observed in the Millennium — and it is observed not just by Israel but by the nations. What does it mean that this feast, pointing to God dwelling with His people, is still being celebrated in the age when God actually does dwell with His people?

Part 3 — The Four Species (Arba Minim)

On the first day of Tabernacles, Leviticus 23:40 commands Israel to take four plants and rejoice before the Lord for seven days. These are known as the Four Species (Arba Minim): the etrog (citron fruit), the palm branch (lulav), myrtle branches, and willow branches. Jewish tradition developed an elaborate theology around each species, binding three of them together (palm, myrtle, willow) and holding the etrog separately.

Leviticus 23:40 (NASB 1995)
'Now on the first day you shall take for yourselves the foliage of beautiful trees, palm branches and branches of trees with thick branches and willows of the brook, and you shall rejoice before the LORD your God for seven days.'
The Traditional Symbolic Interpretation
Jewish tradition assigned meaning to each species based on its characteristics:
• Etrog (citron) — has both taste and fragrance. Represents those who have both Torah knowledge and good deeds. Held upside down (representing human sin) at the start; by the end of the seven days it is held right-side up (representing redemption).
• Palm (lulav) — produces edible fruit but has no fragrance. Represents those who have Torah knowledge but lack good deeds.
• Myrtle — has fragrance but no edible fruit. Represents those who do good deeds but lack Torah study.
• Willow — no taste, no fragrance. Represents those who have neither Torah knowledge nor notable deeds — yet they are still bound together with the whole. No Israelite is discarded.
Held Together — The Unity of the Body
The midrash teaches that by binding all four species together, no Israelite is left behind. The one who has everything (etrog) stands together with the one who has nothing (willow). They are waved together — east, south, west, north, up, and down — as a declaration that God's presence fills all of creation, and that His people, in all their diversity and spiritual condition, are brought before Him together. This carries a powerful New Covenant echo: the body of Christ is not composed only of the spiritually mature. The weak are bound to the strong.
🙏 Reflection
The willow — no taste, no fragrance — still belongs in the bundle. God does not discard those who feel they have little to offer. In what ways does this picture speak to how God sees those who feel spiritually empty, or how the church should hold together the weak and the strong?

Part 4 — The DSS Calendar and the Feast Calendar

One of the recurring themes of this study is that the Dead Sea Scrolls Essenes preserved a 364-day solar calendar that placed every feast on a fixed day of the week, every year — a design they believed was given by God and that the Pharisees had abandoned. The feasts, in this framework, were not approximate observances. They were precision appointments embedded in the very structure of time.

Tabernacles on the Essene Calendar
On the 364-day solar calendar, the Feast of Tabernacles always begins on the 15th of the seventh month — and because the calendar is fixed, that day always falls on the same day of the week. There is no drift, no recalculation, no moon-watching required. The feast structure is:
• 1st day of Tabernacles: solemn assembly, no ordinary work
• Days 2–7: daily decreasing burnt offerings (72 bulls total over seven days in Numbers 29)
• Day 8: the Shemini Atzeret — the Eighth Day Assembly, a separate feast
The Essenes saw this as proof that God designed time as a prophetic instrument. Every feast pointed to a fulfillment, and the precision of the calendar guaranteed that those fulfillments would land on exact divine appointments — not floating dates.
The Calendar Dispute and Its Prophetic Stakes
If the DSS calendar reflects the original design — and multiple scholars believe it predates the Pharisaic lunar-solar calendar — then the question is not just historical but prophetic: are the feasts being observed on God's intended days? The teaching in this session reinforced a conviction already in this study: the Pharisees' introduction of the observation-based lunar calendar in the post-exilic period may have systematically shifted Israel's feast dates away from their original divine appointments. The implications for prophetic fulfillment are significant and remain an area of ongoing study.

🌿 Special Study: The Willow in Scripture — For Hazelee Willow

A Personal Note
During the teaching session that generated this module, a question was raised: "I keep hearing about willows. My granddaughter Hazelee Willow always comes to mind. I want to know the relevance of the willow — willow tree, willow branches, willow bush — and make it a separate study."
This section is dedicated to that question and to Hazelee Willow. Names matter. And in Scripture, the willow carries a beauty all its own.

The Willow in the Feast of Tabernacles (Leviticus 23:40)

As covered in Part 3, the willow is one of the Four Species. The willow — no taste, no fragrance — represents the person who feels they have nothing to offer. Yet they are bound together with the etrog, the palm, and the myrtle, and they are waved before God together. The willow is not excluded. It is an essential part of the bundle. You cannot fulfill the commandment without it.

The Name Meaning
The willow tree is known in Hebrew as aravah (עֲרָבָה). The root carries connotations of a brook, a stream, or a desert plain. Willows grow at the water's edge — they need water to survive, and they mark the presence of water even in dry lands. In Scripture, willows at streams are a sign of life, abundance, and restoration.
Psalm 137:1–2 (NASB 1995) — Willows by the Waters of Babylon
By the rivers of Babylon, there we sat down and wept, when we remembered Zion. Upon the willows in the midst of it we hung our harps.
The Willows of Grief and the Willows of Restoration
Psalm 137 is one of the most heartbreaking passages in the entire Bible — Israel in Babylonian exile, unable to sing the Lord's song in a foreign land, hanging their harps on the willows. The willow becomes a symbol of mourning, of silence, of loss. But this is not the end of the willow's story in Scripture.
🎯 Important NASB upgrade: In the NLT the Hebrew aravah was translated as "poplar trees" — but in the NASB 1995 it is correctly translated as "willows." Israel literally hung their harps on the willows in their grief and exile. This is a direct textual link to Hazelee Willow's name.
In Isaiah 44, God promises a great restoration — and the willow appears in that restoration:
Isaiah 44:3–4 (NASB 1995) — Restoration and Growth
'For I will pour out water on the thirsty land and streams on the dry ground; I will pour out My Spirit on your offspring and My blessing on your descendants; and they will spring up among the grass like poplars by streams of water.'
From Psalm 137 to Isaiah 44 — The Full Arc
The willows in Psalm 137 hold the harps of a silent, weeping people. The poplars/willows in Isaiah 44 are thriving, flourishing — fed by the outpouring of God's Spirit. The same imagery of water-loving trees appears in grief and in glory. This is a picture of the full journey of God's people: through exile, through silence, through loss — and into a restoration that exceeds the original joy.
A child named Willow carries something of this arc: the tree that grows at the water's edge, that appears in Israel's deepest mourning, and that God uses as the very image of His people thriving when His Spirit is poured out. That's a beautiful name to carry through life.
⚠️ Translation note: Isaiah 44:4 — NASB 1995 reads "poplars by streams of water" while NLT reads "willows on a riverbank." Both are rendering the same water-loving tree imagery; the Hebrew words for willow and poplar overlap significantly in ancient usage. The theological point — God's people thriving beside streams of His Spirit — is preserved in both translations.

Willows in Job — The Image of Resilience

Job 40:22 (NASB 1995) — Describing Behemoth
The lotus plants cover him with shade; the willows of the brook surround him.

Even in the poetry of Job — the most ancient of the wisdom books — willows appear as markers of a lush, life-bearing environment. They cluster at the water's edge, providing shade, shelter, and cover.

Ezekiel's Willow — New Growth

Ezekiel 17:5 (NASB 1995) — The Parable of the Eagles
He also took some of the seed of the land and planted it in fertile soil. He placed it beside abundant waters; he set it like a willow.

In Ezekiel's allegory of Israel's kings and empires, the willow is the image of a transplanted seedling growing strong in a new place. God takes what has been uprooted and plants it in good soil to flourish. Again — the willow as a picture of restored life, not just mourning.

Summary: What the Willow Means
Across Scripture, the willow carries at least four distinct meanings:
1. Presence at the water — it grows where there is life-giving water; it marks God's provision
2. Inclusion in the bundle — bound with the whole people before God, never discarded even when it seems to offer little
3. Grief held faithfully — the tree that held Israel's harps in exile; grief that does not mean abandonment
4. Thriving restoration — the tree Isaiah uses to picture what it looks like when God pours out His Spirit
To be named Willow is to be named for a tree that appears in grief and in glory, that grows at the water's edge, that is never left out of the bundle, and that God Himself uses as the picture of His people flourishing in the Spirit.
