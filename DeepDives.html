Prophetic Calendars: The 360-Day Year vs. the 364-Day Jubilees Calendar

Daniel · Revelation · Genesis · Dead Sea Scrolls · What calendar does God use in prophecy?

⚠️ Why This Matters
Every end-times timeline you read — the 70 Weeks of Daniel, the 1,260 days of Revelation, the 42 months of the Tribulation — depends on what kind of year you're counting with. If the prophetic year is 360 days (not 365 and not 364), then the math changes. So does your understanding of when these events begin and end.

The Three Calendars in Play

CalendarYear LengthNotes
Modern Solar365.25 daysOur standard calendar. Not used in biblical prophecy calculations.
Jewish Lunar~354 days (12 months × 29.5)Traditional Hebrew calendar. Adds a 13th month (Adar II) every few years to realign with the sun.
Jubilees / Enoch Calendar364 days (52 weeks exactly)Used by the Dead Sea Scrolls community (Qumran). Always begins on a Wednesday. Feasts always fall on the same day of the week.
Prophetic Year360 days (12 months × 30)Used in Daniel, Revelation, and implicitly in Genesis's Flood. The calendar God appears to use for end-times reckoning.

The Key Problem: 1,260 Days

The number 1,260 appears multiple times in Daniel and Revelation as the length of the second half of the Tribulation. Scripture also expresses this same period three different ways:

Three Ways Scripture Says the Same Thing
1,260 days — Revelation 11:3; 12:6
42 months — Revelation 11:2; 13:5
"A time, times, and half a time" — Daniel 7:25; 12:7; Revelation 12:14 (= 1 + 2 + 0.5 = 3.5 years)
These three expressions all point to the same 3.5-year period. The math only works cleanly with a 360-day year: 3.5 × 360 = 1,260. And 1,260 ÷ 42 = 30 days/month.
The Jubilees Calendar Doesn't Work Here
1,260 ÷ 364 = 3.4615... (not a clean number)
1,260 ÷ 365.25 = 3.449... (not a clean number)
1,260 ÷ 360 = 3.5 exactly ✓
Only the 360-day prophetic year produces a clean result. This strongly suggests Scripture is not using the Jubilees calendar or the modern solar calendar for its prophetic timelines.

The Evidence for a 360-Day Prophetic Year

1. Genesis — The Flood Account

Genesis 7:11, 24 & 8:3–4
The flood began on the 17th day of the 2nd month and the waters receded on the 17th day of the 7th month — exactly 5 months. Genesis 7:24 says the waters prevailed for 150 days. Therefore: 5 months = 150 days → 1 month = 30 days → 1 year = 360 days.

This is the earliest evidence in Scripture of a 30-day month. God's own narration of the Flood uses this calendar — and it predates both the Jewish lunar calendar and the Jubilees calendar.

2. Daniel — The 70 Weeks

Sir Robert Anderson's landmark 1894 calculation in The Coming Prince demonstrated that Daniel's 69 weeks (483 prophetic years) — calculated at 360 days per year — pointed with remarkable precision to the day of Christ's triumphal entry into Jerusalem. Using a 365.25-day year produces a result that misses by years. Using 360 days lands within the correct week.

Anderson's Calculation (Simplified)
69 weeks × 7 years/week = 483 prophetic years
483 × 360 days = 173,880 days
From the decree of Artaxerxes (Nisan 1, 445 BC) + 173,880 days = Nisan 10, 32 AD — the date of the Triumphal Entry
Note: Scholars debate the exact starting date. But the 360-day year produces a far more precise result than any other calendar.

3. Revelation — Consistent Use Throughout

Revelation 11:2–3
"...they will trample the holy city for 42 months. And I will appoint my two witnesses, and they will prophesy for 1,260 days..."

In back-to-back verses, Revelation uses both "42 months" and "1,260 days" for what appears to be the same period. 42 × 30 = 1,260. This is only consistent if months are 30 days — the 360-day year. John appears to use these interchangeably as a deliberate signal that the prophetic calendar operates on 30-day months.

The Jubilees Calendar — What It Is and Why It Matters

The Book of Jubilees (also called "Lesser Genesis") was a Second Temple Jewish text highly prized by the Qumran community, the group responsible for the Dead Sea Scrolls. It retells Genesis through Numbers using a strict 364-day solar calendar: 4 seasons × 13 weeks × 7 days = 364 days. Every year begins on a Wednesday (the day God created the sun and moon in Genesis 1).

Why the Jubilees Calendar Is Significant
364 days = exactly 52 weeks, meaning every feast day falls on the same day of the week every year. Proponents argue this was God's original intended calendar — perfect, orderly, and tied to creation week. The Qumran community believed the Jerusalem temple priesthood had adopted the wrong (lunar) calendar, corrupting the feast schedule.
Why It Does Not Appear to Be the Prophetic Calendar
Despite its elegance, the 364-day calendar does not produce clean results when applied to Daniel's 1,260 days or the 70 Weeks. It also drifts from the actual solar year by 1.25 days/year — meaning it would fall out of alignment with the seasons over time without intercalation.
The Relationship Between the Two
Some scholars (including scholars of the Dead Sea Scrolls) suggest the 364-day calendar and the 360-day prophetic year are related but serve different purposes: the 364-day calendar as Israel's sacred/festival calendar during certain periods; the 360-day year as God's divine reckoning for prophetic timelines. They are not in conflict — they serve different functions.

Scholarly Positions

Sir Robert Anderson (1894) — The Coming Prince
Foundational work demonstrating that 69 weeks of Daniel × 360 days = the precise date of Christ's triumphal entry. Established the 360-day prophetic year as the standard framework for dispensational prophecy. Still widely cited today.
Key text: Daniel 9:24–27; Luke 19:41–44
Harold Hoehner — Chronological Aspects of the Life of Christ
Updated Anderson's work with refined dating. Confirmed the 360-day year framework produces a historically defensible result for the 69 weeks calculation, though with slightly adjusted calendar endpoints.
Ron Rhodes — The End Times in Chronological Order
Accepts the 360-day prophetic year as the standard for reading Tribulation timelines. Treats 1,260 days = 42 months = 3.5 years as a consistent framework throughout Daniel and Revelation.
Relevant to your current reading
Dead Sea Scrolls Scholars (e.g., VanderKam, Wise)
Document the Jubilees/Enoch calendar as the calendar used at Qumran. Argue it may represent an ancient priestly calendar predating the lunar calendar. Do not specifically address whether this calendar applies to prophetic timelines.

Open Questions for Further Study

🔬 Questions This Deep Dive Raises
If the 360-day prophetic year is correct, does it affect how we calculate the start and end of the Tribulation in actual calendar terms? Could we — or should we — try to calculate approximate dates?
The Flood account in Genesis uses a 30-day month. Does this mean the pre-Flood world operated on a different calendar than today — possibly before the Earth's orbit or rotation shifted? Some creation scientists argue this.
The Book of Jubilees is not canonical Scripture. But it was clearly valued at Qumran and may reflect a genuine ancient priestly tradition. How much weight should non-canonical Second Temple literature carry in our interpretive framework?
Does Sir Robert Anderson's calculation — pointing to the Triumphal Entry — constitute proof that the 360-day year is God's intended prophetic calendar? Or is it a remarkably precise approximation that works out close enough to be compelling without being definitive?
If the Jubilees calendar was intended for feast scheduling and the 360-day year for prophetic reckoning, what calendar should we use for our own liturgical and devotional life? Does it matter?
