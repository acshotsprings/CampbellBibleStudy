Bible Translations

Each translation serves a different purpose. The NLT is the default for this guide.

NLT Default
New Living Translation
Thought-for-thought in modern English. Prioritizes clarity and readability. Used throughout this entire study guide.
Best for: Reading large passages and devotional study.
ESV
English Standard Version
Word-for-word, balancing accuracy with readability. Widely trusted for in-depth study and theological precision.
Best for: Cross-checking key prophetic words against NLT.
KJV
King James Version
The 1611 Authorized Version. Highly literal and poetic. Classic prophecy teachers (Walvoord, Lindsey, Scofield) quote KJV.
Best for: Cross-referencing classic commentaries.
NASB
New American Standard Bible
One of the most literal translations. Preferred by seminaries. Excellent for word studies in Daniel, Ezekiel, and Revelation.
Best for: Word-level accuracy in prophetic passages.
ASV
American Standard Version
Classic 1901 literal translation. The base text your current Ezekiel 36–37 / Romans 9–11 / Zechariah 12 read-through is using.
Best for: Reading without verse numbers for flow.
AMP
Amplified Bible
Expands key words using brackets to show the full range of Hebrew and Greek meaning. Useful for prophetic word depth.
Best for: Understanding depth in specific prophetic words.
LXX
Septuagint (Greek OT)
Greek translation of the Hebrew OT used by the early church and quoted in the New Testament. Important for how Ezekiel was read by NT writers.
Best for: Tracing OT prophecy as read by NT writers.

Ancient Texts — PDF Resources

Second Temple and inter-testamental texts. Not Scripture but historically significant. PDFs are stored in your resources folder and open directly.

📜 The Book of Enoch
1 Enoch — Standard English Version, Jay Winter translation · ~2nd–1st century BC · Found in Dead Sea Scrolls · Quoted in Jude 1:14–15
Ancient Jewish apocalyptic text with visions of fallen angels, the coming judgment, the "Son of Man" figure, and detailed astronomical/calendar material relevant to Theme 2. Quoted directly in the New Testament by Jude. Not canonical but illuminating for prophetic and calendar background.

📜 The Book of Jubilees
R.H. Charles translation · ~2nd century BC · Found in Dead Sea Scrolls · Treated as authoritative at Qumran
Retelling of Genesis and Exodus organized around a 49-year jubilee cycle. Insists on a 364-day solar calendar and condemns the lunar calendar. Foundational for understanding the Essene calendar conviction and its prophetic implications. Directly ties to Ken Johnson's reading on the Biblical Calendar bibliography page.
→ Theme 2 Module 3 (dedicated module), Module 1 (Daniel's 70 Weeks), Theme 2 Module 1 (Calendar History)

📚 The Talmud — Mishnah & Gemara
Sefaria.org · Complete Babylonian Talmud in English · Searchable by tractate · Free and open-access
The central text of rabbinic Judaism — the Mishnah (oral law written down ~200 AD) plus the Gemara (rabbinic commentary and debate). Not Scripture, but indispensable historical context for how Second Temple and early rabbinic Jews actually understood and practiced Torah. The tractate Yoma covers Yom Kippur — the Day of Atonement in Leviticus 16 — with detailed procedural commentary (the two goats, the red thread, the high priest's garments, the scapegoat sent to Azazel). Essential companion for any serious study of the feasts, temple ritual, or Jewish calendar background.
→ Theme 2 Module 4 (Yom Kippur & Tabernacles — Mishnah Yoma detail) · Biblical calendar context · Second Temple Judaism
