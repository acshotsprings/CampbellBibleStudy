Module 5 — Theme 2: Biblical Calendar & Prophecy

The Hebrew Calendar

The twelve months, the seven feasts, and why God's timing still matters

Read first
Genesis 1:14 · Exodus 12:1–2 · Leviticus 23:1–44 · Colossians 2:16–17

Every other module in Theme 2 — Calendar History, Israel in Prophecy, the Book of Jubilees, the Feast of Tabernacles — assumes you already know what a Hebrew month is, which feasts God commanded, and when they fall on His calendar. This module is the foundation under those modules. It's not a deep dive into disputes over which calendar is correct (see the Prophetic Calendars Deep Dive for that). It's the plain teaching: here is how God structured time, here are the feasts He appointed, and here is why they still matter for prophecy today.

Genesis 1:14 (NASB 1995)
"Then God said, 'Let there be lights in the expanse of the heavens to separate the day from the night, and let them be for signs and for seasons and for days and years.'"
The word "seasons" is not what you think
The Hebrew word translated "seasons" in Genesis 1:14 is mo'adim — appointed times, fixed meetings, sacred assemblies. It's the same word used later for the feasts of the LORD in Leviticus 23. From the fourth day of creation, God built His appointed calendar into the sun, moon, and stars. Time itself is designed for worship.

Part 1 — The Two Calendars of Israel

Israel keeps two parallel calendars — not in conflict, but serving different purposes. Once you understand this, a lot of confusion about "when is the Jewish New Year?" disappears.

The Religious (Sacred) Calendar
First month: Nisan (spring, roughly March–April). Established by God at the Exodus. This is the calendar of the feasts, of prophecy, of God's redemptive timeline.
Exodus 12:1–2 (NASB 1995)
"Now the LORD said to Moses and Aaron in the land of Egypt, 'This month shall be the beginning of months for you; it is to be the first month of the year to you.'"
The Civil (Agricultural) Calendar
First month: Tishri (fall, roughly September–October). Rooted in the agricultural year — harvest, reset, jubilee. The modern Jewish New Year (Rosh Hashanah) falls on 1 Tishri on this calendar, even though Scripture counts Nisan as month one.
Which one does Scripture use for prophecy?
The religious calendar. When Daniel speaks of "seventy weeks," when Revelation speaks of 1,260 days, when Zechariah ties a future outpouring to "the first month" — these all count from Nisan. The civil calendar governs year numbers; the religious calendar governs God's appointments.

Part 2 — The Twelve Hebrew Months

The Hebrew calendar is lunisolar — twelve months of roughly 29–30 days following the moon, with a thirteenth leap month added seven times in every nineteen years to keep the feasts aligned with their agricultural seasons. Here are the twelve months in religious-calendar order (starting with Nisan), with their Gregorian overlap and feast anchors.

Three months carry almost all the prophetic weight
Nisan (Passover cluster), Sivan (Pentecost), and Tishri (Trumpets, Atonement, Tabernacles). The other nine months contain historical commemorations but no feasts commanded by God in Leviticus 23. When prophecy speaks of God's appointed calendar, it is almost always speaking of these three months.

Part 3 — The Seven Feasts of the LORD

Leviticus 23 is the master feast chapter of the Bible. In it, God lists seven mo'adim — appointed times — that Israel is commanded to keep. Four fall in the spring and early summer; three fall in the fall. This spring/fall division is not decorative; it is theological.

Leviticus 23:1–2 (NASB 1995)
"The LORD spoke again to Moses, saying, 'Speak to the sons of Israel and say to them, "The LORD's appointed times which you shall proclaim as holy convocations — My appointed times are these:"'"

Three pilgrimage feasts
Three of the seven feasts — Passover, Pentecost, and Tabernacles — required every male in Israel to travel to Jerusalem to appear before the LORD (Exodus 23:14–17, Deuteronomy 16:16). These are called the shalosh regalim — the three pilgrim feasts. This is why the crowds in Jerusalem were so large during these events in the Gospels.

Part 4 — Why the Calendar Matters Prophetically

Here is the pattern that ties this module to everything else in Theme 2 and Theme 1: Christ fulfilled the four spring feasts at His first coming — on the exact days they were appointed. The three fall feasts remain unfulfilled. If God kept His appointed calendar for the first four with that level of precision, it is reasonable to expect the last three will unfold the same way.

The spring pattern — fulfilled on the day
• Passover (Nisan 14) — Christ was crucified on Passover (1 Corinthians 5:7–8).
• Unleavened Bread (Nisan 15) — His sinless body lay in the tomb during the feast of sinlessness.
• Firstfruits — He rose on the day Israel waved the firstfruits sheaf before the LORD.
• Pentecost (Sivan 6) — fifty days later, the Spirit was poured out on the exact day Israel celebrated the giving of the Law at Sinai (Acts 2:1–4).
Four feasts, four exact fulfillments. The calendar was not symbolic — it was prophetic, to the day.
The fall pattern — still waiting
• Trumpets (Tishri 1) — a shofar blast to announce the King. Many pre-trib teachers see this as the moment of the Rapture (1 Thess 4:16–17), unknown in the day or hour (Matt 24:36–44) — fitting, because Trumpets was the one feast that began at the sighting of the new moon, which no one knew exactly.
• Day of Atonement (Tishri 10) — Israel mourns, looks on Him whom they pierced, and the nation is cleansed in a single day (Zech 12–13).
• Tabernacles (Tishri 15–21) — God tabernacles with His people in the millennial kingdom. Zechariah 14:16 says all nations will come up to Jerusalem yearly to keep this feast.
Zechariah 14:16 (NASB 1995) — Tabernacles in the Millennium
"Then it will come about that any who are left of all the nations that went against Jerusalem will go up from year to year to worship the King, the LORD of hosts, and to celebrate the Feast of Booths."
The gap between spring and summer is not empty
Between Pentecost (Sivan 6) and Trumpets (Tishri 1), there is roughly a four-month gap with no commanded feast. This is sometimes called the "age of the Gentiles" or "the church age" in prophetic teaching — the season we are living in now. The harvest has been in motion since the first Pentecost; the trumpet has not yet sounded.

Part 5 — The New Testament Lens

If God built the feasts into creation itself and Christ fulfilled four of them to the day, a fair question follows: are Christians required to keep the feasts? Paul answers this directly in Colossians 2, and his answer is the key to reading this whole module without drifting into legalism.

Colossians 2:16–17 (NASB 1995)
"Therefore no one is to act as your judge in regard to food or drink or in respect to a festival or a new moon or a Sabbath day — things which are a mere shadow of what is to come; but the substance belongs to Christ."
Shadow and substance
Paul does not say the feasts were wrong, meaningless, or obsolete. He says they are a shadow — and shadows are real, cast by something real. The feasts cast the shadow of Christ forward through Israel's history. He is the substance — the body that cast the shadow. Shadows are not the point once the substance has arrived, but they are still instructive — they tell us the shape and timing of what God has done and what He will still do.
Practical conviction
This module is not a call to observe the Hebrew feasts as a Christian requirement. It is a call to understand them — because they are God's own prophetic calendar, and seeing how precisely He kept them in the past is a reason for confidence that He will keep them in the future. Passover morning was not a coincidence. Pentecost fire was not a coincidence. The Trumpet will not be a coincidence either.

Reflection Questions

God tied His appointed times to the sun and moon in Genesis 1:14 — long before Israel existed. What does it tell me about God that He wrote worship into the structure of creation itself?
Christ fulfilled four feasts on the exact calendar day they fell. If God kept those appointments with that precision, how does that shape my trust in the fall feasts still to come?
Paul said the feasts are a shadow of Christ, who is the substance. How do I hold the tension between honoring what God appointed and not slipping into legalism about observing it?
The gap between spring and fall feasts is the season we live in — harvest time before the trumpet. How should that shape my urgency in sharing the gospel with the people God has placed in my daily life?
Holy Spirit, is there anything in how I currently mark time — my weeks, my seasons, my rhythms — that You are inviting me to re-anchor in Your calendar instead of the world's?
